Piyush Pandey

The Man Who Wrote for India (1955-2025)

The Architect of Indian Advertising

Piyush Pandey didn‚Äôt just create advertisements; he created India's collective memories. He was the man who took the polished, anglicized voice of Indian advertising and replaced it with the 'mitti ki khushboo' (the scent of the soil), the wit of the streets, and the warmth of our homes. He passed away on October 24, 2025, at 70, leaving behind a four-decade legacy at Ogilvy that didn't just sell products but gave a nation its voice.

He proved that you didn't need to look to Madison Avenue for ideas when you had the bus stands of Jaipur. He showed that "Hinglish" wasn't a compromise but a richer, more authentic language. He turned "boring" products like glue (Fevicol) and paint (Asian Paints) into beloved storytellers, and chocolate (Cadbury) into an expression of adult joy.

This tribute is an exploration of his genius‚Äîhis deep insights, his iconic work, his infectious humor, and the profound legacy he leaves behind.

The Ad-Sutra (His Insights)

Piyush's genius wasn't just creativity; it was a profound philosophy. He didn't just *use* "Hinglish"; he *was* "Hinglish."

Insight 1: The "Anti-Translation" Principle

Before Pandey, ads were often conceived in English and then poorly translated into Hindi. Pandey argued that translation is "the biggest enemy of communication." His revolution was to *think* in Hindi, capturing the language's cadence, idioms, and wit, making ads instantly authentic and deeply resonant.

Insight 2: The "Non-Adman" Adman

His background as a **state-level cricketer** and **professional tea-taster** was his superpower. It gave him an outsider's empathy and a real-world observation deck, allowing him to see the magic in the everyday lives of ordinary Indians, untouched by ad-school jargon.

Insight 3: "Mahim, not Manhattan"

Even after being promoted to Worldwide Chief Creative Officer in 2019, Pandey famously refused to move to Ogilvy's New York HQ. He insisted on operating from Mumbai. He knew his creative strength came from being immersed in India's culture, close to the "bus stands and chai stalls." He had to protect his source.

Insight 4: Trust Over Process

His legendary, decades-long relationship with clients like Fevicol wasn't built on decks, but on mutual trust and friendship. He presented ideas on gut, proving that disruptive work comes from brave clients who trust instinct over research. As he said about Fevicol, the client relationship was the "biggest lottery ticket."

Insight 5: The Power of Simplicity

He famously believed, "If my mother can understand an ad without effort, I know it's a good one." He cut through jargon and complexity, focusing on simple, human truths that resonated universally, regardless of language or background.

Insight 6: Teamwork Makes the Dream Work

He saw creativity as a team sport. "Build a community of friends... who possess different talents," he wrote in *Pandeymonium*. Icons like the Hutch Pug and ZooZoos were team efforts nurtured under his leadership, proving his ability to scale craft through collaboration.

Insight 7: Storytelling, Not Selling

He saw advertising's role as connecting, not just convincing. "Advertising should leave you happier than before you saw it," he believed. He sold emotions ‚Äì laughter, pride, nostalgia ‚Äì embedding brands into the fabric of life, making them feel like old friends rather than products.

Iconic Ads (The Backstories)

Piyush didn't just make ads; he made folklore. Here are the richer stories behind the legends, filled with unique facts and anecdotes.

1. Cadbury Dairy Milk ‚Äì ‚ÄòKuch Khaas Hai‚Äô (1993/94)

The Brief: In the early 90s, chocolates were strictly for kids in India. Cadbury needed adults to embrace Dairy Milk. The market wasn't growing.

The 'Pandey-sian' Insight: Give adults "permission" to indulge by tapping into the universal joy of celebration and the "child in each one of us". The line "Kuch Khaas Hai" aimed to capture the *feeling* of the moment.

The Legend (Spontaneity Rules!): Authenticity was key. Pandey insisted on **no choreographer** for model Shimona Rashi's iconic dance. "We just said you do what you feel like," he recalled. Out of 13-14 takes, they used the **very first one** because it felt the most real. Pandey even wrote initial English lyrics before adapting the jingle perfectly into Hindi with Shankar Mahadevan.

The Impact: Revolutionized the chocolate market in India for adults. It bridged generations, loved by teens and their nostalgic mothers alike. Pandey summed it up: "The 90s‚Äô ad was so much from the heart... My book is in my heart".

2. Fevicol ‚Äì ‚ÄòThe Fevicol Ka Jod‚Äô Universe

The Brief: Transform Fevicol, an industrial adhesive‚Äîa "commodity product"‚Äîinto a brand with emotional connection and top-of-mind recall.

The 'Pandey-sian' Insight: Don't sell glue; sell the *unbreakable bond* ("Fevicol ka Jod"). Use hyper-local, wordless humor rooted in everyday Indian scenarios (like overcrowded transport) to make the message universal.

The Legend (Trust & Speed): The client relationship was legendary. The Impact: Turned a functional product into enduring Indian folklore. The "Bus" ad became cultural shorthand.

3. Asian Paints ‚Äì ‚ÄòHar Ghar Kuch Kehta Hai‚Äô (2002 onwards)

The Legend (His Own Voice): The iconic, warm, slightly gravelly, and deeply authentic voice-over in the original campaign wasn't a professional artist. **It was Piyush Pandey himself.** This unconventional choice made the campaign feel incredibly personal and relatable, bypassing the usual ad-speak polish.

The Impact: Gave a voice to the walls of a home, turning paint from a commodity into a canvas for life's stories.

The Brief: Create a memorable identity for Hutch in India's booming, cluttered early-2000s telecom market.

The Man Behind the Moustache

His legendary status wasn't just built on campaigns, but on his larger-than-life personality, infectious laugh, and deeply human approach. His work was full of humor because *he* was full of life. He found gold in the most ordinary places.

The Cannes Bus Story: Pride in Roots

Prasoon Joshi recalled being on a bus at the Cannes Lions festival, surrounded by international delegates. Out of nowhere, Piyush started humming a Rajasthani folk song. Then, one by one, he encouraged the entire bus‚Äîpeople of all nationalities‚Äîto join in. A moment showcasing his immense, unapologetic pride in his roots and his ability to connect with anyone, anywhere.

The SBI Life Ad: Real-World Magic

CCO Sridhar shared how Piyush's SBI Life Insurance ad (an elderly man buys a diamond for his wife who questions the age appropriateness) inspired his *own* father, who had never done so, to buy a diamond for his mother. The husband's reply in the ad, "Does the diamond know your age?" wasn't just a line; it sparked real-life action. Sridhar called Piyush immediately to share the magic.

The Ogilvy Standard: Setting the Bar High

A former Ogilvy creative remembered his boss nervously pitching a "safe," conventional script to Piyush in the lobby. Piyush listened quietly, paused, and delivered the knockout punch: "We are Ogilvy. We cannot present this," and walked off. A terrifying but career-defining lesson in creative ambition and upholding the agency's legacy.

The Hugger & The Booming Laugh: Leadership with Heart

Colleagues uniformly recall that "he never shook hands; he always hugged." His laugh was famously loud, booming, and filled the room before he even entered it. It wasn't just a sound; it was an energy. His leadership style was built on an embrace, warmth, and genuine connection, not hierarchy or fear.

Blending Business and Friendship: The Client Connection

Deepak Iyer (Mondelez) noted Pandey's unique ability to blend client relationships with deep, genuine friendship. Sudhanshu Vats (Pidilite) cherished their monthly "Coffee Pe Charcha" (Discussion over Coffee) at Piyush's home, discussing life, culture, and brands with equal passion. This deep trust fueled decades of iconic, risk-taking work.
